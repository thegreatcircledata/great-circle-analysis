Figure 1. Temporal layering of corridor activity
Five of six tested epochs from 130,000 BP to present show enrichment of sites along the Great Circle, with the single gap at Last Glacial Maximum (26,000-14,000 BP) consistent with paleoclimate models of Arabian hyperaridity. Z-scores and p-values indicate significance level for each epoch.

Figure 2. Out-of-Africa Southern Dispersal Route fit
The Great Circle (defined by pole at 59.682122°N, 138.646087°W) overlaid on the consensus southern dispersal route from East Africa through Arabian Peninsula to South Asia and Southeast Asia, showing mean distance to route waypoints at 96th percentile of latitude-matched random great circles (p = 0.042).

Figure 3. Deep-time site enrichment
Geographic distribution of 40 archaeological sites dated >40,000 BP along the proposed southern dispersal route. Sixteen sites (40%) fall within 500 km of the Great Circle (Z = 4.42, p = 0.001), including Mololo Cave (12 km) and Kebar Valley (16 km).

Figure 4. Agricultural domestication centers
Twenty-four independent centers of plant and animal domestication (Larson et al. 2014) with nine falling within 500 km of the Great Circle, spanning three continents (zebu cattle, taro, llama/alpaca, potato, wheat/barley). Circle passes closer to these centers than 99.8% of random great circles (p = 0.002).

Figure 5. Least-cost path analysis (early Holocene)
Optimal overland routes between Levant, Persepolis, and Mohenjo-daro during the early Holocene (~10,000 BP) computed using SRTM elevation, sea-level adjustment (-40 m), and Green Arabia aridity masks. The Great Circle approximates the southern arc route better than 99.2% of random great circles (p = 0.008).

Figure 6. Memphis complex: Ahramat Branch intersection
The Great Circle crosses the Ahramat Branch (reconstructed Nile tributary) near-perpendicularly at the Memphis necropolis. Fifteen pyramids fall within 10 km of intersection point (29.921°N, 31.149°E); intersection angle varies by scale: 54-70-81-86 degrees (local to full branch). Compound probability 0.00016.

Figure 7. Directional Ripley's K-function (Egyptian pyramids)
All ~138 Egyptian pyramids decomposed into parallel and perpendicular coordinates relative to the Great Circle's local bearing. Extreme directional clustering along circle-parallel axis (ratio 0.08, p < 0.0001) indicates pyramids arranged in linear strip rather than merely clustered near circle.

Figure 8. Temporal decomposition of monument-settlement divergence
Monument-settlement divergence (Z_monument - Z_settlement) computed at 250-year resolution from 3500 BCE to 500 CE. Sharp spike at 2750-2500 BCE driven by 31 Egyptian Old Kingdom pyramids in Memphis necropolis. Predynastic periods show zero on-corridor enrichment before Naqada II (3500 BCE).

Figure 9. Hypothesis elimination matrix
Summary table of fifteen alternative hypotheses (astronomical, geomagnetic, gravitational, mythological, navigational, and cultural-transmission mechanisms) tested against random circle baselines. All return null results, ruling out physical Earth properties and known cultural transmission vectors as explanations for monument clustering.
